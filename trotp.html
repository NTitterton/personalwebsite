Book Review: The Revolt of The Public

21 February 2026

This review is very tardy. I read The Revolt of The Public in November 2025 (sixth, after Scientific Freedom; I write this as I'm on the 15th book), but I need to catch up on reviews and figured I would get this one out of the way.

This one was quite unique: it probably fits the least cleanly into the four categories versus any other Stripe Press book. I would classify it as a history, focusing on the global political environment after ~2010. In one sentence, the book is about how smartphones led to the rise of populism.

Some weaker parts are the summary sections at the end of each chapter, which occasionally read like an impact section or simply obligatory rather than a part-by-part summary, and the aforementioned lack of diagrams. This book, more than others, felt adaptable to a video series or movie.

Here's the summary.

  • Introduction: defining production efficiency; penicillin example from discovery to mass production; economic growth partly comes new tech and more capital, but also more effiency.
  • Chapter 1: What a production process is. (inputs, steps, outputs; process flow diagram); five factors for improvement: transformation method, production rate, inputs and outputs, buffersize, variability; improvements to each factor eventually lead to continous flow processes; bulb example (Corning ribbon machine).
  • Chapter 2: New Proceses. nail manufacturing example (hand forge -> cut nail -> wire nail). New production methods (technologies) as an S-curve: at first initial kinks, then productivity increases, then diminishing returns. Bulb example. Overlapping S curves. Complications to S curve pattern: multiple axes of performance; coupling btwn production method, product deisng, functionality; lack of portability (esp staff training); performance ceilings without a successor technology. Mechanization (turning human labor step to machine-performed) as common new production method (usually by simplifying the step).
  • Chapter 3: Reducing input costs. Redesign the product (value engineering, producibility guidelines, design for manufacturing and assembly (DFMA)); redesign the production process (operations research (OR), japanese single-minute exchange of die (SMED)); changing the organizational structure (vertical integration vs subcontracting; model T, US Steel examples); changing the location (offshoring); increasing the value of outputs (e.g. finding uses for waste products). Trade-offs exists (e.g. large fixed followed by low marginal cost vs the inverse) and steps/technologies can be coupled. Explore vs exploit dynamics in trying new things. Case study: Tesla and large castings.
  • Chapter 4: Production rate and economies of scale. scaling exponent b: C~x^b. mechanisms behind economies of scale: fixed cost spreading; geometric scaling (square-cube law of volume vs surface area, example of container ships); statistical scaling (std deviation scales with sqrt(n) for n parts); influence scaling (getting a better deal with your suppliers); learning curve effects; network effects (e.g. airline networks providing more value with more destinations). Diseconomies of scale: admin costs (coordination of n people takes n^2 connections); demand effects (exhausting all sources of an input, etc); geometric diseconomies (e.g. taller buildings); statistical diseconomies (e.g. larger power plants: n parts of a system where any one failure fails the whole system). Cost-curve shapes (U vs L-shaped). Reliance on scale effects. Mass production and mass consumption (example of railroad leading to proliferation of cast-iron stoves). False economies and false diseconomies: preferable to use produciton methods that don't have economies of scale to avoid risky large fixed costs (lean manufacturing). Bottlenecks and theory of constraints.
  • Chapter 5: Removing a step. Value-add vs non-value-add steps. Removing non-value add: Job analysis, motion study, Gilbreth's therbligs and time systems. Work simplification, scientific management. Toyota production systems, lean methods, poka-yoke, 5 whys. Removing value add: interchangeable parts, Model T paints and Tesla.
  • Chapter 6: Variability, Knowledge, and Control. variation in a step quality, amount of time, etc. Buffer buildups. Reducing variability: history of trade secrets, measuring instruments, scientific explanations of steps, experimentation. Natural variation vs assignable cause variation. Control charts (X-bar vs R charts). Shielding from variation: factory building, machine covers. Compensating for variation: control systems like thermostats, HVAC systems, flyball governors in engines. Toyota production system as variation reduction: fluctuating demand, kanban system, worker suggestions. Making products/processes robust to variation: wide tolerances, combining parts to reduce tolerance stacking, Taguchi methods, pull system instead of push system. Variability tradeoffs with higher costs, trend of less variability over time, good variability, buffers as a shield for fluctuating demand (e.g. Toyota chip buffer after COVID).
  • Chapter 7: Learning Curves. straight, downward lines on log-log plots of cumulative production vs cost-per-unit. Steepness of slope is learning rate. learning effects. Indirect effects (like economies of scale). Artifact effects (time or decreased cost causing quantity increase). All 3 effects often entangle, with volume creating opportunities for improvement. Learning curve can be thought of another way of looking at an S curve. Then Potter models learning curves by considering a production process as a set of sometimes dependent steps that can be improved w/ prob proportional to how slow the step currently is. Few, highly interdependent steps -> slow learning rate. Many, indepent steps -> high learning rate. Disruptions of learning curve (like work holidays or product design changes). Limits to learning curves (everything is a straight line on a log-log chart).
  • Chapter 8: Bundles, Chains, and Feedback Loops. Bundles of improvements can occur with changes to production method, reducing work in process, and standardization (for example). Chains of improvements can occur with reductions in variation, Toyota Production System. Then Potter has a long example on Ford's Model T, where interchangeable Parts, the assembly line, and economies of scale cyclically reduced costs.
  • Chapter 9: Continuous Processes. The end evolution of a production process, where inputs continuously turn into outputs. No buffers. Contrasts with batch processes. Sometimes economies of scale make batching more efficient. Toyota production system, groupt technology try to get best of both worlds. Continuous processes can often be so efficient, just a few machines produce the entire world's supply.
  • Chapter 10: Failures to improve. Improvement can fail if the previous techniques (economies of scale, better production methods, etc) are blocked, or the process accumulates unctrollable costs. Blocked improvements: tech limitations (e.g. titanium), political limitations. Blocked improvement can lead to a negative cycle of poor stability, no learning, no economies of scale. Ex: medical care is very non-uniform. Added production burdens (accumulated costs): regulation, increasing input costs (Baumals Cost Disease). Long case study: building construction: housing construction prices have outpaced inflation: prefab has failed to decreased costs due to difficulty of transport, low volume, cyclical demand, high variability, regulations, increasing labor costs.
  • Conclusion: General theme: economies of scale, and repeatable process. Where will future improvements come from: flexible equipment and information processing (robotics), to solve the last bit of mechanization-resistant steps, and handle variability (e.g. fluid dynamics simulation). Long case study: home construction of the future: design generated in realtime online, humanoid bots with self-driving truck(s) make and build panels on site, robot cameras ensure regulation compliance.

Here are my takeaways.

  • Learning rates require uninterrupted production, working best with small, independent steps. This made intuitive sense and built a nice mental model for why Nuclear Power Plants haven't gotten cheper, but RAM and soybeans (my selections) have. This reminds me of the nice ladder of applications that existed as computation power 10xed each decade via Moores Law (there's a nice chart in The Art of Doing Science and Engeering). I wonder what the ladder would look like for more precise general-purpose manufacturing (aka nanotech).
  • Economies of Scale. Everything becomes easier when fixed costs can be spread and a production process repeated more times. It reminds me of a discussion in What We Owe The Future where the author talks about the hypothetical of the world population equalling Switzerland's: you would see a lot less specialization that would hugely increase poverty. I often wonder about the reverse situation where the world has ~10x population and there are 10x as many (or one 10x as large) canneries, software startups, ASMLs, etc...

One last note: the book sounds dry, but I felt the pathos. Maybe it was all that Factorio I played last year (it's pretty good at contextualizing certain concepts from the book, like buffers and push-versus-pull supply chains). But this stuff matters, and is part of the glorious symphony of spontaneous complexity that has lead to those cheap, cheap crunchwrap supremes I love so much.

I'm now reading Building in Public
